About

I work on system identification for stable LTI systems using adaptive observers, and more broadly on learning in dynamical systems.

I am a Direct-PhD student in the Systems Control Group at the University of Toronto, advised by Mireille E. Broucke. My current work focuses on indirect identification of stable linear time invariant systems via adaptive observer constructions, with an emphasis on stability guarantees and robustness.

Before Toronto, I completed a B.E. in Electrical & Computer Engineering at the American University of Beirut (AUB) with a CGPA of 4.0 and Dean’s Honor List recognition. I also spent a summer as an E3 research intern at the Adaptive Systems Laboratory (EPFL), working on multi-agent reinforcement learning in decentralized, partially observable settings, and I have worked on shared control and human–machine interfaces for autonomous driving.

Beyond research, I enjoy teaching, mentoring, and using data to improve how we design and deliver technical courses.

Research

Themes and questions I am currently interested in.
  • Adaptive observer–based identification. Indirect identification schemes for stable LTI systems that combine adaptive observers with classical system identification tools, with provable convergence and robustness properties.
  • Decentralized learning under partial observability. Policy evaluation and information sharing in decentralized POMDPs, including belief-sharing architectures and communication constraints.
  • Shared control and HMI for automated driving. Driver intention recognition and control handover for stop–and–go scenarios, using simulators and sequence models.
  • Data-informed teaching and learning. Statistical analysis of performance and engagement in large, mixed-format introductory programming courses.
